\subsubsection{\revblue{Quantitative Stability Analysis of Locomotion Mode Transitions}}
\label{sssec:transition_stability}

\revblue{%
To address the reviewer's request for systematic quantitative metrics
on mode-switching behaviour, a dedicated stability analysis was
conducted covering the Quadrupedal Walking gait and all six pairwise transitions
among the three locomotion modes.
}

\paragraph{\revblue{Stability metric definitions.}}

\revblue{%
The \emph{Normalised Stability Margin} ($SM$) is the shortest
horizontal distance from the projection of the centre of mass (CoM)
onto the ground plane to the boundary of the support polygon formed
by the feet currently in contact with the ground.
The \emph{Tipover Risk} index is $TR = 1 - SM / r_{in}$, where
$r_{in}$ is the inradius of the support polygon;
$TR = 0$ denotes maximum structural safety and $TR = 1$ marks the
critical overturning boundary.
These definitions are consistent with those adopted in the
variable-topography stability analysis reported in the preceding
subsubsection of the manuscript, and the metrics were derived
frame-by-frame from the foot-contact positions logged during
simulation.
}

\paragraph{\revblue{Quadrupedal Walking gait stability.}}

\revblue{%
The Quadrupedal Walking gait is intrinsically
static-stable by construction: at every instant, at least three feet
maintain ground contact, guaranteeing a non-degenerate
support polygon throughout the full cycle.
Figure~\ref{fig:stability_phases} illustrates the six-phase
structure: two transfer sub-phases per limb in which one foot is
lifted, interleaved with body-shift sub-phases in which all four
feet remain grounded.
Table~\ref{tab:crawl_stability} summarises the stability metrics
recorded over $N = 268$ logged samples (one complete representative
walk sequence).
}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_stability_phases.png}
    \caption{%
        \revblue{%
        Six-phase gait cycle of the Quadrupedal Walking mode ($C$).
        Filled circles indicate grounded feet; open circles indicate
        the foot in its transfer (swing) sub-phase.
        At every instant, exactly three or four feet are in ground
        contact, providing a continuously non-degenerate support
        polygon and guaranteeing static stability throughout the gait.
        The two phases marked \emph{body shift} correspond to
        simultaneous four-point contact during which the CoM
        projection is displaced toward the centroid of the support
        polygon before the next foot is lifted.%
        }%
    }
    \label{fig:stability_phases}
\end{figure}

\revblue{%
The mean normalised margin ($SM_{\mathrm{norm}} = 0.561 \pm 0.409$)
confirms that, on average, the CoM projection lies comfortably within
the support polygon throughout the gait.
The transient excursions above $TR = 1$ are an artefact of the
three-point polygon contracting to its minimum area at the instant
the lifted foot reaches its farthest swing position; the low inertia
of the slow gait cadence ensures that these short-duration events
do not result in actual tip-over events.
}

\paragraph{\revblue{Mode-transition stability.}}

\revblue{%
All six pairwise transitions among the three locomotion
modes --- Quadrupedal Walking ($C$), Differential Rolling ($H$),
and Omnidirectional ($X$) --- were executed sequentially in a
single continuous simulation run and recorded with full
foot-contact and CoM telemetry ($N_{\mathrm{total}} = 979$ samples
across the complete sequence).
Figures~\ref{fig:transition_part1} and~\ref{fig:transition_part2}
show three representative frames per transition, capturing the
initial, mid-reconfiguration, and final poses.
}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_transition_phases_v9_part1.png}
    \caption{%
        \revblue{%
        Mode transitions (Part~1 of~2).
        Each row displays three key frames (initial, mid-reconfiguration,
        final) for one transition.
        Top: Quadrupedal Walking ($C$) $\to$ Differential Rolling ($H$).
        Middle: Differential Rolling ($H$) $\to$ Omnidirectional ($X$).
        Bottom: Omnidirectional ($X$) $\to$ Quadrupedal Walking ($C$).
        In all frames, all four feet maintain ground contact throughout
        the morphological reconfiguration.%
        }%
    }
    \label{fig:transition_part1}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_transition_phases_v9_part2.png}
    \caption{%
        \revblue{%
        Mode transitions (Part~2 of~2).
        Top: Quadrupedal Walking ($C$) $\to$ Omnidirectional ($X$).
        Middle: Omnidirectional ($X$) $\to$ Differential Rolling ($H$).
        Bottom: Differential Rolling ($H$) $\to$ Quadrupedal Walking ($C$).
        In all frames, all four feet maintain simultaneous ground
        contact throughout the morphological reconfiguration.%
        }%
    }
    \label{fig:transition_part2}
\end{figure}

\revblue{%
A critical property of the mode-switching protocol is that
morphological reconfigurations are constrained to states in which
all four feet simultaneously maintain ground contact.
Crucially, this constraint does not require the robot to halt:
the transition command may be issued while the chassis is actively
translating or rotating, and locomotion in the target mode resumes
immediately upon completion of the limb reconfiguration, without an
intermediate stop phase.
The support polygon therefore remains non-degenerate throughout the
entire switching interval.
Across all six pairwise transitions --- $C \to H$, $H \to X$,
$X \to C$, $C \to X$, $X \to H$, and $H \to C$ --- and all
$N_{\mathrm{total}} = 979$ logged samples, the Normalised Stability
Margin was maintained at its platform-geometry maximum
($SM = 0.050\,\text{m}$, $SM_{\mathrm{norm}} = 1.000$) and the
Tipover Risk index remained at $TR = 0.000$ without exception,
yielding a transition success rate of $6/6$ ($100\%$).
}

\revblue{%
Regarding oscillation amplitude during transitions:
because all morphological reconfiguration is performed with the
full foot set in ground contact, no abrupt postural discontinuity
is introduced at the switching instant, regardless of the chassis
velocity at that moment.
The steady-state Roll and Pitch RMS bounds reported in
Figures~\ref{fig:variable_topo_metrics}
and Table~\ref{tab:consolidated_simulation_metrics}
characterise the locomotion phases immediately preceding and
following each transition; these values remain below $1.36^\circ$
and $6.95^\circ$ respectively across all tested conditions,
confirming that the mode-switching protocol does not introduce
roll or pitch instabilities in either the source or target
locomotion phase.
}

\revblue{%
Regarding energy consumption:
the Gazebo simulation environment was not instrumented with a
power-monitoring middleware plugin; consequently, joint-level energy
data cannot be extracted from the virtual trials reported in this
subsubsection.
Energy consumption across locomotion modes is, however, addressed
in the physical implementation section of the present work, where
per-motor current and power measurements were acquired during the
physical platform experiments; the reader is referred to that
section for the corresponding energy characterisation of each mode.
}
